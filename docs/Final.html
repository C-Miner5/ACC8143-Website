1. A description of your data and where you found it and what you are predicting

The data concerns patients who are at risk for heart disease, and contains multiple attributes that can be risk factors or red flags for heart disease. It was developed by combining five other publicly available heart datasets and eliminating the duplicates. I will be using it to predict if a patient has heart disease.

Source: https://www.kaggle.com/fedesoriano/heart-failure-prediction

2. Descriptive statistics - small discussionare the skewed or are there outliers etc.

The patient records are highly skewed towards men, and towards patients with <= 120 mg/dl fasting blood sugar. The resting BP has a high kurtosis, partly because of one major outlier with no resting BP, and 20 records (~2%) exceeding 180 bpm.

The average patient age is 53, which is useful for this prediction as young people rarely have detectable heart problems on average.

The cholesterol levels vary pretty widely, and I predict will be an important variable.

But overall the dataset is very clean.

3. Describe your machine learning model (short description)

Gradient boosting is very similar to random forests, in that it uses many decision trees to develop a better prediction model. It differs in how the trees are created and how the results are combined. The decision trees are developed iteratively with each new tree helping to fix the errors of the previous tree, to form an optimal model.

4. Split data, run model, and find the results

heart<-heart%>%
  mutate(prediction=if_else(HeartDisease==1,"YES","NO"))

set.seed(1)
#lets split the data 60/40

trainIndex <- createDataPartition(heart$prediction, p = .6, list = FALSE, times = 1)

#grab the data
heartTrain <- heart[ trainIndex,]
heartTest  <- heart[-trainIndex,]

set.seed(1)

heartXGB<- train(
  form = prediction~Age+Sex+ChestPainType+RestingBP+Cholesterol+FastingBS+RestingECG+MaxHR+ExerciseAngina,
  data = heartTrain,
  #here we add classProbs because we want probs
  trControl = trainControl(method = "cv", number = 10,
                           classProbs =  TRUE),
  method = "gbm",
  tuneLength = 20,
  verbose=FALSE)

5. Describe your results. How can this be useful

The results show some strong associations between most of the data elements and heart disease. The accuracy and kappa levels are high, but so high that they indicate a problem with the data or the model.

The false positive and false negative rates are not too high, but in this sort of prediction false negatives can be very dangerous. A patient who has heart disease and is told they do not will probably not get the treatment, or make the lifestyle changes needed to prevent it from getting worse.

The presence of an exercise induced angina and the total serum cholesterol levels were far and away the most important influencers. Atypical angina chest pain, maximum heart rate were also fairly important. Sex (Male) was, though it is possible that the fact that the data skewed heavily towards male patients affected its importance level.

This sort of model can be used by doctors to predict patients that are likely to be at risk of heart disease, so that treatment can begin early before the patients health gets worse.
